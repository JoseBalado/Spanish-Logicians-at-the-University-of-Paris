**Luis Núñez Coronel** was a Spanish philosopher, natural philosopher and theologian born in Segovia around 1480. Together with his brother Antonio, he formed part of the remarkable circle of Spanish and Scottish logicians gathered at the Collège de Montaigu in Paris around John Major. His *Physicae perscrutationes* (Paris, 1511) is one of the most significant works in the history of late medieval dynamics, and he was one of the leading figures in the promotion of Erasmism in Spain.

### Family

Luis was the son of Diego Núñez de León and a woman of the powerful converso family of the Coronel. The Coronel surname had been granted in 1492 to Rabí Mayr Melamed (baptized as Fernán Núñez Coronel) at the time of his conversion to Christianity, and Luis and Antonio were his great-grandchildren.[^1] Their older brother Francisco Fernández Coronel, a distinguished military man, was already in Paris and brought them to Montaigu.[^2]

### Studies at Salamanca and Paris

Luis and Antonio first studied Latin in Segovia and then attended the University of Salamanca for several years. Encouraged by their brother Francisco and aided by letters of recommendation from the Catholic Monarchs, they went to Paris around 1500.[^3]

Luis entered the Sorbonne as a *hospes* (guest) in 1504 and as a *socius* (fellow) in 1509. He was ordained a priest in 1512, received the licentiate in theology on 26 January 1514, and the doctorate on 29 May 1514. García Villoslada called both brothers "the two pillars of Montaigu."[^4]

### Intellectual Work in Paris

In Paris, Luis published the *Tractatus [de formatione] syllogismorum* in 1507 or 1508 and the more substantial *Physicae perscrutationes* in 1511, based on his lectures at Montaigu. He assisted John Major in preparing his *Logic* for publication, alongside his brother Antonio and Gaspar Lax. He also helped Luis Vives with his commentary on Augustine's *City of God*, a project that Erasmus had advised and encouraged.[^5]

The *Physicae perscrutationes* (166 folios in the Lyons edition of 1512) are set within the framework of Aristotle's *Physics*, with long digressions on motion (thirty-five folios in Book III) and on infinity (thirty-eight folios in Book VIII). Coronel's physical doctrines were influenced by Jean Dullaert of Ghent and Álvaro Tomás, and he cites Jean Buridan, Richard Swineshead, Albert of Saxony and Gregory of Rimini with greatest frequency. In the tract on motion he argues that impetus is not really distinct from local motion. On infinity, he holds that God can produce a syncategorematic but not a categorematic infinity, differing on this point from John Major and Juan de Celaya.[^6]

Pierre Duhem, commenting on the significance of this work, noted that Coronel went further than Jean Dullaert in giving his text the form of an original treatise on physics, and that his formulation of impetus anticipates the terminology later used by Galileo (*impeto* or *momento*), Descartes (quantity of motion) and Leibniz (living force). Duhem also observed that the dynamics taught by Dullaert and Luis Coronel at Montaigu, and by Juan de Celaya at Sainte-Barbe, is the dynamics of Jean Buridan and Albert of Saxony — the dynamics of impetus.[^7]

Coronel refutes the arguments of Italian Averroists who opposed this dynamics, and specifically combats the view of Nicolò de Chieti, who held that falling bodies and projectiles are moved by the ambient air. On the motion of wheels, he observes: "this explanation does not account satisfactorily for the rotary motion of a wheel that no one is throwing, which throughout its motion remains in contact with the same air; in such a case one cannot say that the walls of the air come back together after being separated, since while the motion lasts the wheel stays in the same place."[^8]

Both brothers were praised by Damián de Goes among the notable men of Spain, and by their contemporaries more generally. Luis Vives, who had initially been sharply critical of Montaigu, later revised his opinion warmly upon returning to Paris, praising the new spirit he found there.[^9]

### After Paris: Flanders and Spain

At the end of 1517, Luis and Antonio left Paris for the Low Countries. Luis, together with Quintana and on the advice of Glapion, was named advisor to Hulst in the inquisitorial proceedings against Probst and Grapheus at Brussels.[^10] When Charles V returned to Flanders in 1520, the Emperor named Luis and Antonio as his preachers and confessors. Aware of his converso status, Luis was careful to write out his sermons in advance so as to be able to justify himself in the event of any denunciation.[^11]

Antonio then returned to Paris, where he died; Luis returned to Spain, though the precise date is not known.

### Luis Coronel and Erasmism in Spain

Back in Spain, Luis became one of the leading advocates of Erasmism. He served as secretary and close advisor to Alfonso Manrique, Archbishop of Seville and Grand Inquisitor. He was also close to Íñigo López de Mendoza, Bishop of Burgos (to whom the *Physicae perscrutationes* is dedicated), Alfonso Fonseca, Archbishop of Toledo, and the Bishop of Zamora. He traveled across a large part of Spain in defense and promotion of Erasmus, visiting Toledo, Burgos, Barcelona and Palencia.[^12]

The relationship between Luis and Erasmus had developed gradually. Initially, both brothers were suspicious of Erasmus's ambiguous stance toward Luther; it was Luis Vives who reassured them and brought them to admire Erasmus. Vives then informed Erasmus of Luis's esteem, and Erasmus wrote to him in 1522 — a warm letter in which he speaks of his delight at the reports he had received of Luis's character and theological learning, and defends his own conduct in the affair of Luther.[^13]

Luis Coronel was praised by Juan de Vergara, Diego Gracián de Alderete, Pedro Juan Olivar (who called him "the greatest advocate of Erasmus, enjoying the highest authority with the Archbishop of Seville"), and Alfonso Valdés (who called him "the most excellent among theologians").[^14] At the Assembly of Valladolid in June 1527, Luis served as the principal defender of Erasmus, arguing against the distortions of his ideas and pointing to the suppression of offending passages in the new edition of the *Apologies* as evidence of Erasmus's good faith.[^15]

Luis also participated in the case of Francisco Ortiz, sent in secret by Archbishop Manrique to persuade him to retract the famous sermon in which he had defended the imprisoned Francisca Hernández. Luis spoke with Ortiz, urging a simple retraction of the offending words, and told him that to do so would be prudence, and to refuse would be pride.[^16]

Bataillon concludes that, if the written defense of Erasmism belongs to the Valdés brothers, Vergara, Virues and others, the practical advocacy — traveling through Spain, advising bishops, mediating disputes — may be said to have been led above all by Luis Coronel.[^17]

### Abbot of San Isidoro de León

Luis Coronel was presented by Emperor Charles V for the abbacy of San Isidoro de León in 1526, but the appointment was contested at Rome due to his converso background and led to lengthy legal proceedings. Despite never being able to visit the house in person, he maintained warm relations with the canons who came to visit him. He is described in the codices of the Colegiata as "a great theologian and preacher, a pious and charitable man," who had shown great affection for the community.[^18]

He died in 1531, as confirmed by a letter of the Archbishop of Toledo to the Emperor, dated 7 March 1531 at Ocaña. He was buried in the Capilla de Santo Martino at San Isidoro de León, as he had directed. He bequeathed his many books, fifteen marks of silver, and the ornaments of his chapel to the Colegiata, and forty-five marks of silver to Our Lady of La Vega.[^19]

Twenty-five years after his death, the Dominican Fray Domingo de Valtanás still cited both brothers as an example of the integration of conversos, praising them as "the two Coronels, brothers, both most learned and great servants of God."[^20]

## Index of Works

### *Tractatus [de formatione] syllogismorum* (Paris, 1507 or 1508)
Printed by Jean Barbier, Paris, February 1507 or 1508. The title page describes the author as *magister Ludovicus Coronel hispanus Segouiensis* and notes that the book was on sale at the bookseller Dyonisius Roce, at the sign of Saint Martin in the *Via Regia*, near Saint-Jacques. An accompanying poem by Guillermo Piel of Tours praises Segovia for having produced such a man. A copy is held at the Biblioteca Menéndez Pelayo, Santander.

---

### *Physicae perscrutationes* (Paris, 1511)
Full title: *Physicae perscrutationes magistri Ludovici Coronel hispani Segouiensis.* Dedicated to Ignacio de Mendoza (described on the title page as *illustrissimus vir Inachus de Mandocia*). A prefatory epistle by Simon Agobertus of Bourges praises Luis Coronel, who was then teaching philosophy at the Collège de Montaigu. Colophon: a vignette of two skeletons with the inscription *memento finis.*

Known editions:

- Paris: Simon Vincent, 1511. Copies in the Biblioteca de la Real Colegiata de San Isidoro de León, the Biblioteca Menéndez Pelayo (Santander), and a private collection in Seville.
- Lyons: Jacob Giunta (*in vico Mercuriali*), 1512. 166 folios. Copy in the Bibliothèque nationale de France, Paris.
- Lyons: Jacob Giunta (*in vico Mercuriali*), 1530. Full title: *Physicae perscrutationes egregii magistri Ludouici Coronel hispani Segouiensis diligenter castigatae: cum tabula alphabetica sententiarum et questionum nuperrime adiecta.* Copies in the Bibliothèque nationale de France (Paris) and the Biblioteca de la Real Colegiata de San Isidoro de León (four copies).
- Alcalá: Juan Brocario, 1539. The verso of the title page contains a dedication from the editor to the Rector of the University of Alcalá, followed by a reproduction of the letter of Simon Agobertus. Copy in the Biblioteca Nacional de Madrid (R. 27.703).

Pierre Duhem gives numerous excerpts in French translation in *Études sur Léonard de Vinci*, III (Paris, 1913). Hubert Élie provides a lengthy French translation of the tract on infinity in his edition of Jean Mair's *De l'infini* (Paris, 1938), appendix 5.
