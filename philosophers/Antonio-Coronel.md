**Antonio Núñez Coronel** was a Spanish philosopher, logician and theologian born in Segovia around 1480. Along with his brother Luis, he was one of the most prominent Spanish scholars at the Collège de Montaigu in Paris in the early sixteenth century, and a leading figure in the remarkable circle of logicians that gathered there around John Major.

### Family

Antonio was the son of Diego Núñez de León and a woman of the powerful converso family of the Coronel. The Coronel surname had been conferred in 1492 upon Rabí Mayr Melamed (baptized as Fernán Núñez Coronel) at the time of his conversion to Christianity; Antonio and Luis were his great-grandchildren.[^1] Their older brother Francisco Fernández Coronel was a distinguished military man who had already settled in Paris before them and drew them to the Collège de Montaigu.[^2]

### Studies at Salamanca and Paris

Antonio and Luis first studied Latin in Segovia, then attended the University of Salamanca for several years. Attracted by the fame of the Parisian theologians — and helped by letters of recommendation and financial support from the Catholic Monarchs — they set out for Paris around 1500–1504.[^3]

At the Collège de Montaigu, Antonio was enrolled as a *hospes* (guest) in 1508 and as a *socius* (fellow) in 1511. He received the doctorate in theology in April 1516 and was ordained a priest. He eventually became rector of the Collège de Montaigu. García Villoslada called both brothers "the two pillars of Montaigu."[^4]

### Intellectual Formation

Antonio was a disciple of two masters in particular: Peter Crockaert of Brussels, a Thomist, and John Mair (Major) of Scottish origin, the leading figure of Parisian nominalist scholasticism. Antonio annotated some of Mair's books and gave Mair extensive praise in his *In Posteriora Aristotelis*. The book *Consequentiarium* was begun by Mair and completed by Antonio Coronel. When Mair published his *Logic*, he acknowledged the assistance of Antonio and Luis Coronel and of Gaspar Lax.[^5]

Antonio cultivated logic and dialectic in particular, and is described by Carl Prantl, writing a century after his death, as an extraordinarily prolific writer (*ein ausserst fruchtbarer Schriftsteller*) and as a representative of both the *via realium* and the *via nominalium*.[^6] Juan Luis Vives praised his work; Menéndez y Pelayo famously called him a "recalcitrant degenerate scholastic." Antonio dedicated several of his works to his brother Francisco.

### After Paris

At the end of 1517, Antonio and Luis left Paris for the Low Countries, where they served as advisors during the inquisitorial proceedings against Probst and Grapheus at Brussels.[^7] When Emperor Charles V returned to Flanders in 1520, Antonio was named his confessor. He was regarded as a rigorous and devout Catholic — despite his converso origins — and was strongly opposed to the theses of Luther.

In 1521, wishing to write a treatise against Lutheran heresy, Antonio decided to return to Paris. He died there, prematurely, overcome by fatigue.[^8]

## Index of Works

### 1. *Tractatus exponibilium et fallaciarum* (Paris, 1509)
Second edition: Paris (*Pro Dyonisio Roce*), 1511. Copy in the Biblioteca Nacional de Madrid (R. 19.977).

---

### 2. *Quaestiones logicae secundum viam Realium et Nominalium*, hoc est, ad Porphyrii *Praedicabilia* et Aristotelis librum *Praedicamentorum* (Paris, 1509)
Further editions: Salamanca, 1512; Alcalá, 1540. The edition of 1509 concludes with a dedication to the author's brother Francisco Fernández Coronel, praised for his learning, nobility of spirit and military discipline. Copies in the Biblioteca Nacional de Madrid (R. 27.523 and R. 22.318) and the Biblioteca de la Real Colegiata de San Isidoro de León (two copies of the 1518 edition, one of the 1512 edition).

---

### 3. *Magistri Anthonii Coronel, Secobiensis, in Posteriora Aristotelis*, una cum textu a Joanne Argyropilo Byzantio translato, commentaria noviter recognita atque ad amussim emuncta (Paris, April 1510)
Dedicated to his brother Francisco Fernando Coronel in the same terms as the preceding work. Copies in the Biblioteca Nacional de Madrid (R. 16.151, with two further copies) and the Bibliothèque nationale de France (Rés. R. 184(2)).
