**John Mair** (also written *Mair* or *Major*; in Latin *Joannes Majoris*) was a Scottish philosopher, logician and theologian, born about 1467 at Gleghornie in East Lothian, Scotland, and died in 1550. He is included here not as a Spanish logician, but because he was at the very centre of the Parisian circle of Spanish logicians: he was a close friend and colleague of [Jerónimo Pardo](../Jeronimo-Pardo/), and the teacher of [Antonio Coronel](../Antonio-Coronel/), [Luis Coronel](../Luis-Coronel/) and [Gaspar Lax](../Gaspar-Lax/).

### Life

Mair received his early education in Haddington, East Lothian, and in 1492–93 studied briefly at Cambridge before matriculating at the University of Paris in 1493. He studied at the Collège Sainte-Barbe, taking his BA in 1495 and his MA in 1496. He then became a *regent* (student master) in Arts at the **Collège de Montaigu** in 1496, the same college where Jerónimo Pardo taught, and began his theological studies under Jan Standonck. He received his Bachelor of Sacred Theology in 1501 and his doctorate on 11 November 1506, thereafter teaching at Montaigu and at the Sorbonne.

In 1518 he returned to Scotland, becoming Principal of the University of Glasgow, but returned to Paris for further periods of teaching. In 1533 he was made Provost of St Salvator's College at the University of St Andrews, where he died in 1550.

### His connection to the Spanish logicians

Mair's relationship with the Spanish logicians at Paris was deep and lasting:

- **Jerónimo Pardo** was his friend and colleague at Montaigu. After Pardo's death, Mair co-edited and republished the *Medulla Dyalectices* (Paris, 1505) together with Jacobo Ortiz, ensuring Pardo's logic manual reached a wider audience.
- **Antonio Coronel** studied under Mair at Montaigu, where he also worked alongside Peter Crockaert; Mair's influence shaped Coronel's prolific output in both the *via realium* and *via nominalium*.
- **Luis Coronel**, Antonio's brother, also taught at Montaigu during Mair's time there.
- **Gaspar Lax** was likewise among Mair's students at Montaigu, before becoming known as the "Prince of Sophists" and teaching Juan Dolz, Juan de Celaya and others.

Wikipedia's article on Mair sums up his influence: "His influence extended through enthusiastic pupils to the leading thinkers of the day but most obviously to a group of Spanish thinkers, including Antonio Coronel."

### Logic works

Mair was a prolific logician. His logical writings were collected and published in 1505, and he continued to write on logic throughout his career. His principal logic publications include:

- *Inclytarum artium ac sacre pagine doctoris acutissimi Johannis Maioris … in Petri Hyspani summulas commentaria* (Paris, c. 1505) — commentary on the *Summulae logicales*
- *Propositum de infinito* and related treatises on infinity and the logic of terms
- *Terminorum exponibilium tractatus* (Paris, 1501)
- *Inclytarum artium … libri quos vocant Metaphysicorum* (Paris, 1506)
- *Lectures in Logic* (Lyons, 1516)
- *Quaestiones logicales* (Paris, 1528)

His logical work was firmly in the nominalist tradition of Buridan and Ockham, concentrating on the analysis of terms, supposition theory, consequences and the logic of infinity — the same topics treated by the Spanish logicians in his circle.

He also co-edited works of others that are important for this group:

- *Medulla Dyalectices* by Jerónimo Pardo (posthumous edition, Paris, 1505), co-edited with Jacobo Ortiz.

### Broader significance

As a teacher, Mair attracted students from all over Europe. His logical and theological approach — nominalist, technically rigorous, skeptical of humanist stylistic fashions — was exactly the intellectual air the Spanish logicians breathed at the Collège de Montaigu. Through his Spanish students, and through Domingo de Soto and Francisco de Vitoria (who studied under Celaya and Lax), Mair's influence penetrated the School of Salamanca and shaped the development of late-scholastic thought in Spain.
