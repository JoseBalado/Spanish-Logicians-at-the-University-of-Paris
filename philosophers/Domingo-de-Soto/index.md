**Domingo de Soto** (1494 – 15 November 1560) was a Spanish Dominican theologian and logician born in Segovia. He studied philosophy and theology at the University of Alcalá and then at the University of Paris, where he was a student of [Juan de Celaya](../Juan-de-Celaya/) at the **Collège Sainte-Barbe**. It is also through Celaya and [Gaspar Lax](../Gaspar-Lax/) that he connects to the wider circle of [John Mair](../John-Mair/) at the Collège de Montaigu.

He is principally known as one of the founders of international law and of the School of Salamanca. He is included here primarily for his Paris formation and his logic manual, which belongs to the tradition of this group.

### Life

After studying at Alcalá and Paris, Soto obtained a chair in philosophy at Alcalá in 1520. Shortly after, he entered the Dominican order at San Pablo de Burgos (1524) and became professor of dialectics at the Dominican house of studies in Segovia (1525). In 1532 he was appointed to the Dominican chair of theology at the University of Salamanca, where he collaborated with Francisco de Vitoria and Melchor Cano on the theological reforms that shaped the School of Salamanca. He was appointed to the principal chair in 1552 as successor to Cano.

Soto served as imperial theologian at the Council of Trent (1545–1547) and was briefly confessor to Emperor Charles V. He died in Salamanca on 15 November 1560.

### His Paris formation and connection to the Spanish logicians

Soto studied at the Collège Sainte-Barbe in Paris under [Juan de Celaya](../Juan-de-Celaya/), who had himself been taught by [Gaspar Lax](../Gaspar-Lax/) and [John Mair](../John-Mair/) at the Collège de Montaigu. Francisco de Vitoria, his later collaborator at Salamanca, also studied in Paris at this time. Through his teacher Celaya, Soto absorbed the tradition of Parisian nominalist logic — the same tradition that runs through Mair, Pardo, and the Coronels.

Once back in Spain, Soto turned against the *via moderna* and pressed for a return to Aristotle and Thomism, but the logical training he had received in Paris was the foundation on which he built.

### Logic works

Soto's main logical publication belongs directly to the Parisian summulae tradition:

- *Summulae* (Burgos, 1529) — a manual of logic in the tradition of Peter of Spain's *Summulae logicales*, revised and reprinted several times. This is his principal contribution to formal logic.
- *In dialecticam Aristotelis commentarii* (Salamanca, 1544) — a commentary on Aristotle's logical works.

The *Summulae* in particular connects Soto to the teaching tradition that produced the logic manuals of [Fernando de Enzinas](../Fernando-de-Enzinas/) and the works of Gaspar Lax and Juan de Celaya.

### Other major works

- *In VIII libros physicorum Aristotelis quaestiones* (Salamanca, 1545; rev. 1551) — his physics commentary, notable for being the first text to clearly state that bodies in free fall undergo uniform acceleration; Pierre Duhem and William Wallace credited it as a precursor to Galileo.
- *De natura et gratia* (Venice, 1547)
- *De iustitia et iure* (Salamanca, 1553) — his principal work on law and justice, foundational for international law.
- *In IV libros Sententiarum commentarii* (Salamanca, 1557–1560)
