**Juan Lorenzo de Celaya** was a Spanish philosopher, logician and theologian born in Valencia around 1490. One of the most celebrated members of the Parisian nominalist circle, he taught logic and natural philosophy at Paris for nearly fifteen years and earned the title *Doctor Resolutissimus* from his contemporaries. Vicente Muñoz Delgado called him "the enormous Valencian" (*el enorme valenciano*); he ended his career as professor of theology and rector of the University of Valencia, where he was instrumental in restructuring its studies and inspired what his student Juan Ribeyro described as a "Celayan school."

### Early Life and Education

Celaya had already studied humanities in his native city when he arrived in Paris around 1505 to follow the arts course. He enrolled at the Collège de Montaigu, where he was able to hear John Major (Mair), and where he certainly had Gaspar Lax as his teacher: Celaya refers to Lax explicitly as *regentis mei* in one of his texts.[^1] He completed his arts degree and obtained the licentiate in 1509.

### Teaching in Paris: Collège de Coqueret (1510–1515)

Once licensed and Master of Arts, Celaya took up a post as reader of philosophy at the Collège de Coqueret from 1510 to 1515. Among his students there were Domingo de Soto and Francisco de Vitoria.[^2] His reputation grew so large that the direction of the Collège Sainte-Barbe feared losing all its Spanish students to Coqueret and to Montaigu, where Gaspar Lax taught, and made determined efforts to secure one of those two professors for its own college.[^3]

### Teaching in Paris: Collège Sainte-Barbe (1515–1524)

In 1515 Celaya moved to the Collège Sainte-Barbe, where he held the chair of philosophy while pursuing his theological studies. He received the licentiate in theology in 1522 and the doctorate on 21 June of the same year.[^4] Among the students who gathered around him was the Portuguese Juan Ribeyro, who became his most ardent disciple and propagandist; in a letter prefaced to Celaya's *Summulae*, Ribeyro asked: "Who, in the name of immortal God, from the whole company of peripatetics, would dare to deny that he has been surpassed by that great Gaspar [Lax of Sariñena], prince of sophists? Which Athenian school or series of academics has had anyone clearer or worthier than our Doctor Celaya?"[^5]

Beyond his teaching, Celaya was appointed Vicar General of several French bishoprics and Abbot of San Andrés in the diocese of Elvense (Elne).[^6] He was already a beneficiary of the Valencia Cathedral from 1517.

During his entire stay at Paris, Celaya was a prolific writer, producing a large number of works on logic and expositions of Aristotle's *Physics* (Paris, 1517), *De caelo* (Paris, 1518), *De generatione* (Paris, 1518), and the *Posterior Analytics* (Paris, 1517). His commentary on the *Ethics* appeared in Paris in 1521.

### The *Expositio in octo libros physicorum*

The *Physics* commentary (Paris, 1517) is Celaya's most important work for the history of science. The treatment of motion in Book III spans seventy-one of the 201 folios of the volume. It surveys the main contributions of the English Mertonians, the Paris terminists, and the Paduan *calculatores*, as well as the teaching then current at Paris in the nominalist (Ockhamist), realist (Scotist), and Thomist schools. Pierre Duhem discussed this work at length in his *Études sur Léonard de Vinci* as a key instance of the transmission of impetus dynamics from the medieval calculators to the early modern science of motion. William A. Wallace has analyzed it as a crucial link in that transmission.[^7]

Following a dispute with the German Germain Wain, Celaya returned to Valencia around 1524.

### Return to Valencia

Celaya's ever-growing fame — which had earned him the title *Doctor Resolutissimus* — brought him honors and positions beyond the lecture hall. Back in Valencia, his fellow citizens pressed him to remain. Juan Andrés Strany, Rector of the University of Valencia from 1521 to 1524, had worked persistently to bring him back; the city council recorded that "mestre Joan Celaya és fill de la present ciutat e home de molta sciència y virtut y doctor gran de París lo qual si en esta ciutat aturava lo Studi general floraria en arts en virtuts y bona criança."[^8]

In 1525 Celaya was appointed professor of theology, and in 1526 rector in perpetuo of the University of Valencia. He introduced extensive reforms: student discipline was regulated, professors' attendance was monitored, students were required to pass examinations before moving from Grammar to Logic, and from 1547 competitive examinations were required of all candidates for professorships. He himself described his Valencia publications as "read in all gymnasia, both privately and publicly, for their clarity, their ease, and finally for the fitting order in which all things are placed."[^9] His commentaries on the four books of the *Sentences* appeared in Valencia between 1528 and 1531. He died in Valencia on 6 December 1558.

### Philosophical Profile

M. Luisa Roure describes Celaya's thought as marked by a pronounced preference for eclecticism — *d'une grande liberté de pensée et d'expression* — within a fundamentally nominalist framework. In natural philosophy, Celaya defends the impetus doctrine in the manner of Jean Buridan and Nicolas Oresme. With Scotus he defends the univocity of being with respect to God and creatures. He expresses admiration for Aquinas but departs from him on the principle of individuation, refusing to hold that an accident is individuated by its subject of reception. Roure's summary: *sans exclure à l'endroit de Saint Thomas une vénération qui n'était pas feinte, mais qui n'allait pas jusqu'à l'adhésion à ses doctrines.*[^10]

Celaya himself declared the independence of his teaching in the prologue to his *Summulae* (Paris, 1515): "Praeter philosophum, id est veritatem ipsam, et Petrum Hispanum, hispanorum decus et columen, imitari volui neminem" (Aside from the Philosopher himself — that is, truth — and Peter of Spain, glory and pillar of the Spaniards, I have wished to imitate no one).[^11]

María L. Roure emphasizes the importance of Celaya as a witness to the doctrinal movement of the first quarter of the sixteenth century and the notable influence he exercised on the Parisian schools through the originality of his work, particularly in logic.[^12]

Works are arranged by date of first edition. Works printed at Paris during the Paris period (1510–1523) are followed by their Valencia and Caen reprints where known; works printed at Valencia are listed separately.

### *Dialecticae introductiones* / *Termini* (Paris, c. 1512)
